Above 17 kHz, human hearing falls off — but microphones don't. Ultrasonic beacons are embedded in TV ads, retail stores, and websites to track you across all your devices at once. Your phone hears them. So does this.

Your phone's microphone — even if the screen is off — hears the beacon and reports back to the same ad server. ▶ how does this work?

Most microphones capture up to 22 kHz (the Nyquist limit at 44.1 kHz sample rate). Human speech is 80 Hz–8 kHz. This experiment listens only to the 17–22 kHz band — the part you can't hear.

Known ultrasonic signals in this band:

  • 18.0–18.5 kHz — Silverpush beacon range (TV ad tracking)
  • 19.0–19.5 kHz — ultrasonic pest repellers (drive away rodents)
  • 20.0–20.5 kHz — Google Nearby API (device pairing)
  • 21.0 kHz — Lisnr (proximity marketing)
  • 17.5 kHz — some hearing test tones / youth alarm "mosquito"

On most phones, the microphone rolls off above 20 kHz. Detection above 21 kHz depends on hardware.
